## What Is Cash Runway (And Why Should You Care)?

Cash runway is the amount of time your startup can operate before running out of money. It's calculated by dividing your current cash balance by your monthly burn rate—the net cash you're spending each month.

Here's why this number deserves your attention:

- **Fundraising timing**: Most rounds take 3–6 months to close. You need to start raising before you're desperate.
- **Strategic decisions**: Knowing your runway helps you decide when to hire, cut costs, or pivot.
- **Investor confidence**: Founders who know their numbers signal competence and control.
- **Peace of mind**: There's something calming about knowing exactly where you stand.

I speak with founders every week who estimate their runway as "about a year" without ever opening a spreadsheet. Don't be that founder. Precision matters.

## The Simple Math Behind Runway Calculations

At its core, calculating runway is straightforward:

```
Cash Runway (months) = Current Cash Balance ÷ Monthly Burn Rate
```

But the devil is in the details. Your **monthly burn rate** isn't just your expenses. It's:

**Gross Burn** – All cash leaving your account (salaries, rent, software, marketing)

**Net Burn** – Gross burn minus any revenue or incoming cash

If you're generating revenue, your net burn is what matters. A startup spending $50,000/month but making $20,000/month has a net burn of $30,000—not $50,000.

### Don't Forget These Hidden Costs

When calculating your burn rate, founders commonly miss:

- **Annual software subscriptions** amortized monthly
- **Upcoming tax payments** (GST, PAYE, provisional tax in New Zealand)
- **One-off expenses** like legal fees or equipment purchases
- **Founder salary adjustments** you plan to make
- **Seasonal fluctuations** in revenue or costs

## When Should You Start Raising Your Next Round?

The golden rule: **Start fundraising when you have 12 months of runway remaining.**

Here's why:

- **Month 1–2**: Prepare materials, update deck, warm up investor relationships
- **Month 3–4**: Active pitching and meetings
- **Month 5–6**: Due diligence, term sheets, legal documentation
- **Buffer**: Deals fall through. Having 6 months of cushion prevents desperation

If you're in New Zealand, factor in additional time. The local investor ecosystem is smaller, and many Kiwi founders successfully raise from Australian or global investors—which adds complexity and time.

## Red Flags: When Your Runway Calculator Reveals Trouble

Certain patterns in your runway calculation should trigger immediate action:

### The Flat Line

Your runway isn't decreasing because you're not growing—or worse, you're treading water while competitors sprint ahead. This is a strategic problem masquerading as a financial one.

### The Cliff

Runway looks fine for 8 months, then drops sharply due to a known large expense (tax bill, contract renewal, planned hire). This is manageable if you see it coming. Many founders don't.

### The Optimism Gap

Your projected runway keeps shrinking each month because revenue isn't hitting targets. This pattern usually means your projections need a reality check.
