# How to Hire Developers for Your Startup: A Non-Technical Founder's Complete Guide

You've got the vision, the market validation, and maybe even some early funding. But there's one gap that keeps you awake at night: you need to build the product, and you don't know how to code.

Hiring your first developer as a non-technical founder feels like navigating a foreign country without speaking the language. Technical jargon flies over your head. You're unsure how to evaluate skills you don't possess. And every mishire costs precious runway you can't afford to waste.

The good news? You don't need to become a programmer to hire great ones. You need a framework—and that's exactly what this guide provides.

## Why Hiring Developers Is Different From Other Roles

Most founders have hired before. Salespeople, marketers, operations staff—you can evaluate their work because you understand the outputs. But software development is different.

Code quality isn't immediately visible. A developer can ship features quickly while building technical debt that cripples your product months later. Conversely, a methodical developer who seems "slow" might be crafting an architecture that scales beautifully.

This opacity creates two common failure modes:

1. **Hiring based on personality** rather than demonstrated capability
2. **Delegating the entire hiring decision** to a technical advisor without understanding the trade-offs yourself

Neither approach serves your startup well. You need to be informed enough to ask the right questions and confident enough to make the final call.

## Where to Find Quality Developers

### Job Boards and Platforms

General platforms like LinkedIn and Indeed work, but you'll compete with thousands of other postings. For better results, consider:

- **AngelList/Wellfound** — Specifically for startup roles; developers here often accept equity-heavy compensation
- **Stack Overflow Jobs** — Developers actively engaged in the community
- **GitHub Jobs** — Though now retired, browsing GitHub profiles directly remains valuable
- **We Work Remotely** and **RemoteOK** — If you're open to distributed teams
- **Hacker News "Who Is Hiring"** — Monthly thread featuring startup-friendly developers

### Referrals and Networks

Your best hires often come through warm introductions. Reach out to:

- Other non-technical founders who've successfully built technical teams
- Investors and advisors in your network
- Technical meetups and startup communities (even if you attend as an observer)
- [Fractional CTO services](TODO: link to fractional CTO article) who often know available talent

### Recruitment Agencies

Specialised tech recruiters can accelerate your search, particularly for senior roles. The fee (typically 15-25% of first-year salary) is worthwhile if it reduces time-to-hire for a critical position. Look for agencies with startup experience—they understand equity compensation and the unique appeal of early-stage companies.

## Evaluating Developers Without Technical Expertise

This is where most non-technical founders struggle. Here's how to assess candidates effectively:

### Review Their Portfolio

Ask for:

- **GitHub profiles** — Look for consistent contributions, well-documented code, and projects similar to yours
- **Live products** — Apps or websites they've built that you can actually use
- **Case studies** — Descriptions of problems they solved and how they approached them

Even without reading code, you can evaluate clarity of communication, attention to user experience, and evidence of completed projects.

### Use Practical Assessments

Replace abstract coding challenges with tasks relevant to your actual product:

- Give them a small, paid project (8-16 hours) to solve a real problem you're facing
- Ask them to review your existing codebase or technical documentation and explain their observations
- Request they diagram how they'd architect a specific feature you need

These assessments reveal how they think, communicate, and approach unfamiliar problems—often more valuable than leetcode-style puzzles.

### Check References Differently

Don't just verify employment. Ask previous managers and colleagues:

- "How did they handle requirements that changed mid-project?"
- "Can you describe a time they had to simplify a complex technical concept for non-technical stakeholders?"
- "Would you hire them again? Why or why not?"
- "How did they perform when working with minimal supervision?"

The answers reveal professionalism, communication skills, and self-direction—critical qualities in early-stage startups.

### Bring in Technical Validation

You don't need to assess technical skills alone. Options include:

- **Paid technical interviews** — Services like Karat or interviewing.io provide structured technical assessments
- **Technical advisors** — Engage a [fractional CTO](TODO: link) or senior developer on an hourly basis to evaluate final candidates
- **Pair programming sessions** — Have a trusted technical contact observe them working through a problem

The key is using external validation as input to your decision—not outsourcing the decision entirely.

## Interview Questions That Reveal What Matters

### On Technical Decision-Making

- "Tell me about a time you chose a simpler solution over a more elegant one. What drove that decision?"
- "How do you decide when to use existing libraries versus building from scratch?"
- "What's your approach to handling technical debt?"

These questions reveal pragmatism and business awareness—qualities that separate good developers from great startup developers.

### On Communication and Collaboration

- "Explain [technical concept relevant to your product] as if I'm completely new to technology."
- "How do you typically share progress and blockers with non-technical team members?"
- "Describe a disagreement you had with a product manager or founder. How was it resolved?"

### On Startup Realities

- "What attracts you to an early-stage startup versus a larger company?"
- "How comfortable are you with ambiguous requirements and shifting priorities?"
- "Tell me about a project where you had to ship something imperfect to meet a deadline."

## Red Flags to Watch For

Certain patterns should give you pause, even if the candidate seems technically impressive:

- **Dismissing your business constraints** — Developers who insist on "doing things right" without acknowledging startup realities often struggle to ship
- **Inability to explain their work** — If they can't describe what they built and why, collaboration will be painful
- **No questions for you** — Good developers want to understand the technical challenges, team structure, and technical decision-making authority
- **Over-engineering examples** — Solutions far more complex than necessary suggest they may optimise for interesting problems over business outcomes
- **Frequent job changes without progression** — In startups, you need people who commit and grow with the company

## Compensation: Salary, Equity, and Alternatives

### Market Rates

Developer salaries vary dramatically by location, experience, and specialisation. In 2026, expect:

- **Junior developers**: $60K–$90K NZD (or local equivalent)
- **Mid-level developers**: $90K–$140K NZD
- **Senior developers**: $140K–$200K+ NZD
- **Specialised roles** (AI/ML, blockchain, security): Premium of 20-50%

Remote hiring can reduce costs, but consider timezone overlap and communication overhead.

### Equity Considerations

Early-stage startups often supplement below-market salaries with equity. Typical ranges for first technical hires:

- **First engineer/CTO**: 2-8%
- **Senior engineer (pre-seed/seed)**: 0.5-2%
- **Mid-level engineer**: 0.1-0.5%

Use a tool like the [Holloway Guide to Equity Compensation](https://www.holloway.com/g/equity-compensation) or Carta's equity calculator to ensure fairness and understand dilution implications.

### Alternatives to Full-Time Hiring

If you're not ready for a permanent hire, consider:

- **Freelancers/contractors** — Platforms like Toptal, Arc, and Gun.io vetted developers for project-based work
- **Development agencies** — Higher cost but reduced management overhead; good for [MVP development](TODO: link)
- **[Fractional CTO services](TODO: link)** — Part-time technical leadership to guide strategy and contractor management without full-time commitment

These options let you validate your product and refine your technical needs before making permanent hiring decisions.

## Setting New Hires Up for Success

Hiring is only half the battle. Non-technical founders must create conditions where developers can thrive:

### Clear Communication Channels

Establish how you'll share requirements, provide feedback, and resolve questions. Written documentation beats verbal instructions—create a shared repository of decisions and context.

### Defined Decision-Making Authority

Be explicit about what developers can decide independently versus what requires your input. Nothing frustrates technical talent like feeling blocked on minor decisions.

### Technical Roadmap Collaboration

Include your developers in product planning. They'll identify risks, suggest alternatives, and feel invested in outcomes. This collaboration also builds your technical understanding over time.

### Regular Check-ins

Schedule weekly one-on-ones focused on blockers, feedback, and professional development. Early-stage developers often wear multiple hats—these conversations surface issues before they become resignations.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Hiring too senior too early** — Senior developers expect mature processes and teams. Sometimes a hungry mid-level developer grows faster with your company.

2. **Optimising for speed over quality** — The "move fast and break things" mentality works until broken things cost you customers. Balance velocity with maintainability.

3. **Ignoring culture fit** — Technical skills can be taught; alignment with your startup's pace, values, and communication style cannot.

4. **Making the first technical hire your CTO** — Lead developers and CTOs require different skill sets. Consider whether you need an executor, an architect, or a leader.

5. **Failing to plan for scale** — Your first hire's choices become the foundation everything else builds upon. Ensure they understand the product vision 12-24 months ahead.

## When to Consider a Technical Co-Founder Instead

Sometimes hiring isn't the right path. If your startup is fundamentally technology-driven—if the tech *is* the competitive advantage—a technical co-founder may serve you better than an employee.

A co-founder brings:

- Skin in the game through significant equity
- Long-term commitment aligned with your timeline
- Technical authority that attracts other technical talent
- Shared decision-making burden

The trade-off is giving up substantial ownership and finding someone with both technical capability and founder temperament. ## Conclusion

Hiring developers as a non-technical founder is challenging but entirely achievable. The key is acknowledging what you don't know while taking ownership of what you do: your product vision, your business constraints, and the cultural environment you're creating.

Focus on finding developers who communicate clearly, demonstrate pragmatism, and show genuine interest in your problem space. Use structured assessments and external validation to compensate for your technical blind spots. And remember that your first technical hire shapes your company's engineering culture for years to come—choose thoughtfully.

You don't need to write code to build a great technical team. You need curiosity, clear expectations, and the humility to ask questions until you understand the answers. Start there, and you'll find developers who don't just write code—they build your vision.
