# The Non-Technical Founder's Complete Guide to Hiring Your First Developer

You've validated your idea, sketched out your vision, and maybe even landed your first paying customer. Now comes the moment every non-technical founder dreads: hiring your first developer. Suddenly you're navigating a world of tech stacks, GitHub profiles, and coding assessments—all while hoping you don't make a six-figure mistake.

This guide will walk you through exactly how to find, evaluate, and hire your first technical hire without pretending to know things you don't. Whether you're building an MVP or scaling beyond your [fractional CTO arrangement](TODO: link), these principles will help you make confident decisions.

## Know What You're Actually Hiring For

Before posting a job ad or reaching out to recruiters, get crystal clear on what this person needs to accomplish. "Build the app" isn't specific enough.

### Define the scope, not just the title

Are you looking for someone to:
- Build a functioning MVP from scratch in the next three months?
- Take over an existing codebase and add features?
- Set up your technical foundation and eventually build a team?
- Handle both development and infrastructure/DevOps work?

Each scenario requires different skills. A brilliant frontend developer who crafts beautiful React interfaces may struggle to architect a scalable backend. Someone who thrives in greenfield projects might feel stifled maintaining legacy code.

### Document your requirements in plain English

Write down what success looks like for this role in your first 90 days. For example:

- "Launch a working mobile app that allows users to create profiles and book appointments"
- "Integrate our payment system with Xero and reduce checkout errors by 50%"
- "Audit our current security practices and implement two-factor authentication"

This clarity helps you communicate with candidates and gives you objective criteria for evaluation.

## Where to Find Developers in New Zealand

The local tech talent market is tight, but that doesn't mean great developers are impossible to find. You just need to look in the right places.

### Local channels that actually work

- **NZ Tech Guild and community meetups** — Wellington's JavaScript meetup, Auckland's Full Stack group, and Christchurch's developer communities are full of talented engineers open to new opportunities
- **Summer of Tech** — If you can wait until the internship cycle, this program connects you with emerging talent
- **Referrals from other founders** — The best hires often come through your network. Ask founders who've built technical teams for introductions
- **Trade Me Jobs and Seek** — Still relevant for local hiring, though you'll compete with larger companies

### Global talent, local context

Remote hiring opens your talent pool dramatically. Platforms like Arc, Toptal, and even well-crafted LinkedIn outreach can connect you with developers worldwide. If you go this route, look for candidates with experience working asynchronously and communicating clearly in writing—skills that matter more than timezone overlap.

## How to Evaluate Technical Skills (Without Being Technical)

Here's the uncomfortable truth: you can't directly assess someone's coding ability if you don't code. But you can absolutely evaluate whether they can deliver results.

### The portfolio review

Ask candidates to walk you through a project they're proud of. Listen for:

- **Problem-solving over features** — Do they talk about the user problem they solved, or just list technologies used?
- **Trade-offs and decisions** — Great developers explain why they chose approach A over B, especially when both had merits
- **Their specific contribution** — In team projects, what did they actually build versus what was built around them?

### The paid trial project

This is your most powerful evaluation tool. Design a small, scoped task that mirrors real work:

- Build a simple feature in your existing codebase (or a similar open-source project)
- Fix a documented bug
- Review and refactor a piece of code, explaining their thinking

Pay candidates fairly for this work—typically a few hours at market rates. The investment is worth avoiding a bad hire.

### Technical references that matter

When checking references, ask specific questions:

- "Walk me through a time they had to figure out something they didn't initially know how to do"
- "How do they handle vague requirements or changing priorities?"
- "Would you hire them again for a project similar to ours?"

Avoid generic questions like "Were they a good developer?"—you'll get generic answers.

## Red Flags Non-Technical Founders Should Watch For

Some warning signs are obvious. Others are subtler but just as costly.

### Communication gaps

Developers who can't explain their work in terms you understand will create ongoing friction. If every explanation devolves into jargon you don't follow, or if they seem frustrated by your questions, that's a preview of your working relationship.

### Over-engineering tendencies

Watch for candidates who propose complex architectures for simple problems. Your first developer should bias toward shipping working software, not building the perfect system. Premature optimization has killed more startups than technical debt ever has.

### No questions about the business

Developers who don't ask about your users, your market, or why you're building what you're building are likely to optimize for interesting technical problems rather than business outcomes. The best technical hires think like product people first.

## The Interview Process That Actually Works

Structure your interviews to reveal how candidates think and work, not just what they know.

### Round 1: The problem-solving conversation (45 minutes)

Present a real challenge your business faces—something you've actually thought through. Ask how they'd approach it. You're not looking for the "right" answer; you're looking for structured thinking, awareness of constraints, and curiosity about details.

### Round 2: The collaborative session (60 minutes)

Bring in a technical advisor or [fractional CTO](TODO: link) if you have one. Have the candidate review actual code (with sensitive details removed) or design a small system with your advisor's guidance. Watch how they handle feedback and alternative suggestions.

### Round 3: The culture and values fit (30 minutes)

Discuss how they prefer to work, how they handle disagreement, and what environment helps them do their best work. Be honest about your company's current state—not the version you aspire to be.

## Setting Your First Developer Up for Success

Hiring is only half the battle. How you onboard and manage your first technical hire determines whether they thrive or leave within a year.

### Clear expectations and autonomy

Define what "good" looks like, then give them room to figure out how to get there. Micromanaging technical work you don't understand destroys trust and motivation.

### Regular communication rhythms

Establish weekly one-on-ones focused on progress, blockers, and alignment—not status updates they could send in an email. Use these conversations to build your own technical literacy over time.

### Technical mentorship

Even senior developers need peers to bounce ideas off. If you don't have technical leadership in-house, consider a [fractional CTO arrangement](TODO: link) or mentorship through networks like NZ Rise or the Developer Society.

## When to Consider Alternatives to Hiring

Sometimes the best first developer is no full-time developer at all. Before committing to a permanent hire, honestly assess whether:

- A no-code or low-code solution could get you to validation faster
- Freelancers or agencies could build your MVP more cost-effectively
- Your current [MVP development approach](TODO: link) actually requires full-time technical staff

Many successful startups began with founders using Bubble, Webflow, or similar tools to prove demand before writing custom code. Don't let technical hiring become a premature distraction from market validation.

## Conclusion

Hiring your first developer as a non-technical founder feels daunting because it *is* consequential. A great first technical hire accelerates everything. A poor one drains resources and momentum.

Focus on clear requirements, practical evaluation through paid trials, and honest communication about what you know and don't know. The developers worth hiring will respect your directness and help fill the gaps in your technical knowledge—not exploit them.

Your first developer relationship is a partnership, not a transaction. Choose someone who wants to build something meaningful with you, and then give them the context, autonomy, and support to do their best work.
